A consulting firm walked into a regional clinic last year with a deck. The deck said the agent could *triage incoming patients, suggest treatments, and flag medication interactions*. The clinic's medical director asked one question: "Who signs the chart?" The deal didn't go forward.

That question is the line. On one side is documentation. On the other is decision. Healthcare agents that respect the line ship. Ones that don't end up in a presentation about "the future of medicine" and never see a real patient.

## What works: the scribe pattern

The most-deployed agent pattern in healthcare today is the AI scribe. The doctor wears a small mic. The patient and doctor talk normally. The scribe captures the conversation, structures it into a SOAP note, and writes it back to the EHR. The doctor reviews and signs.

The scribe doesn't decide anything. It documents. The decision-maker — the licensed clinician — stays the decision-maker. That preserves the chain of accountability, which is what makes the scribe survive a deposition, a malpractice review, an audit. It also makes the scribe a 50-minute-per-shift time-saver, which is what makes clinicians actually use it.

We've built and shipped scribes into psychiatric clinics, pediatric urgent cares, and dental practices. Same pattern every time. The scribe drafts. The clinician owns.

## What doesn't work: the nurse pattern

Pilots that try to make an agent *act* on the medical decision side keep failing. The pattern looks innocent — "the agent suggests a medication adjustment for the doctor to approve." But the moment a clinician approves something the agent surfaced, two things happen:

1. The clinician's review behavior shifts toward the suggestion. (Anchoring. Well documented.)
2. The agent's failure becomes the clinician's signature.

That's a regulatory problem. It's also a malpractice problem. And it means clinics with risk-averse counsel won't deploy the agent at all, no matter how good its medical reasoning is.

The nurse pattern can probably work eventually — with FDA clearance, dedicated eval discipline, and a different liability model. But it's not the work of a Tuesday afternoon agentic pilot. Most teams treating it that way are about to have a hard conversation with the risk officer.

## The four conditions for healthcare agents

If you're building one for industry, four conditions are non-negotiable:

**1. The licensed clinician owns the chart.** No exceptions. The agent's output is a draft. The signature is human.

**2. PHI never leaves the controlled boundary.** Whether that means an on-prem inference setup, a HIPAA-compliant vendor, or a redaction layer — pick one and audit it.

**3. There's a complete trail.** Every input, every output, every model version, every prompt version, every reviewer's edit — logged and retrievable for at least the period required by your jurisdiction.

**4. The eval set has clinician input.** A scribe that drafts notes is graded on what *clinicians* find useful, not on what an LLM judges as well-structured. The eval discipline has to include clinical reviewers.

## Where the line might move

The line will move. There are categories of medical decision where agents will eventually be trusted to act — radiology assists, scheduling, prescription renewals for stable chronic conditions. The path there is not "ship a more confident pilot." It's accumulating eval data, getting regulatory clearance category-by-category, and earning trust the way every previous medical technology has — slowly, with paperwork, with skeptics.

The teams that respect that timeline will own the next decade of healthcare AI. The teams that try to leapfrog it will end up writing post-mortems.

## Close

Healthcare agents work when they document. They fail when they decide. The scribe yes / nurse no test is unglamorous, but it's how the work survives the parts of healthcare that aren't about technology — the audits, the deps, the bedside conversations, the families.

If you're building a healthcare agent, write down which side of the line every output sits on. If anything sits on the decision side, ask who signs.
