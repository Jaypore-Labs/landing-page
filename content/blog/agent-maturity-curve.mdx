Every other week a team tells us they have *agents in production*. Half the time they mean a Slack bot that uses an LLM. The other half they mean a tool-using system that's writing into the company's general ledger. Both are real. They're not on the same planet.

Before we run a series on agents in industry, we need a shared map. So here it is — five rungs of agent maturity, ordered by what's actually at stake.

## The five rungs

**Rung 1 — Pilot.** A single engineer or small team has an agent running in a sandbox. Maybe it's hooked to a real LLM, maybe a tool or two. There's no reviewer, no eval set, no on-call. The agent's output goes into a Notion doc or a Slack channel. **Stakes: zero.** Failure is invisible. Most "agent demos" live here forever.

**Rung 2 — POC.** Same agent, now running for a few real users. The team has *some* logging and *one* manual reviewer. There's a prompt-versioning convention, even if it's just a Git branch. The agent output influences a human decision but doesn't *make* one. **Stakes: low.** Failures are caught by the reviewer or the user.

**Rung 3 — Scoped production.** The agent has a real owner, an eval suite running in CI, and a defined scope. The output is shipped to users without a manual reviewer in the loop, but only inside a narrow lane (e.g., "summarise this support ticket," not "send this email"). There's a kill switch. **Stakes: medium.** Failures degrade UX or burn money quietly.

**Rung 4 — Owned production.** The agent has a product manager. It has SLOs (latency, cost, quality). It runs against a regression cohort that gets added to weekly. There's a runbook. There's an on-call. The agent does take some terminal actions — sends emails, creates tickets, posts updates. The blast radius is bounded by tool design. **Stakes: high.** Failures show up in support, in dashboards, in invoices.

**Rung 5 — Retired.** The agent is shut down. Either the workflow outgrew it, the model behind it changed and recovery wasn't worth the effort, or — most often — the team realised the underlying need was solved by a deterministic pipeline plus a tiny model. **Stakes: zero again.** What matters is that the team learnt something and wrote it down.

## What teams plateau on

Most teams in industry sit at Rung 2. They have a few POCs, none of which has earned the budget for an eval suite, an owner, or an on-call. The thing that pushes a team to Rung 3 is rarely technical. It's a stakeholder who says "I'd rather have one agent that's actually owned than five that someone vaguely hopes work." The thing that pushes a team to Rung 4 is even rarer: an outage that nobody owned, followed by the discipline to never let that happen again.

This series is mostly about Rungs 3 and 4 — agents that real industries are betting on. We'll write about healthcare, legal, finance, manufacturing, retail, and the rest in turn. For each industry we'll ground the article in a specific deployment, with the eval-set design, the kill-switch design, and the reviewer pattern. The boring milestones.

## How to know which rung you're on

Four questions. If you can answer "yes" to all of them, you're at Rung 3 or above. If not, you're at Rung 2.

1. **Does the agent have a named owner who can revert it within an hour?**
2. **Is there an eval set that runs in CI on every prompt change, with a real pass/fail gate?**
3. **Is there a kill switch wired into the production deploy?**
4. **Are failures detectable by something other than a customer email?**

A surprising number of "agents in production" fail at question 4.

## Close

Agents are not a binary thing — "in production" or "not in production." They're a curve. Most teams telling you they have agents in production are at a different point on the curve than the team next door. The interesting work happens between Rung 3 and Rung 4. The boring milestones are what gets you there.
