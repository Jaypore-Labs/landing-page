LLM-as-judge is the eval pattern where a model grades another model's output. It's powerful — it scales eval to dimensions humans can't easily encode. It's limited — judges have their own biases.

## The judge contract

A well-designed judge:

- Has a specific rubric (not "is this good?").
- Returns a structured score (1-5, with rationale).
- Is calibrated against human scores.
- Is audited periodically.

Without these, the judge is unreliable.

## Calibration

The calibration:

- Sample 100 cases.
- Have humans score them.
- Have judge score them.
- Measure agreement.
- Iterate the judge prompt until agreement is acceptable (typically 80%+).

A judge whose agreement with humans is poor isn't ready.

## Reviewer ritual

Judge outputs reviewed periodically:

- Sample of judge scores re-reviewed by humans.
- Disagreements investigated.
- Judge prompt iterated.

## Where judges shine

- Open-ended outputs (prose, summaries).
- Multi-dimensional rubrics (tone, completeness, accuracy).
- High-volume cases where human eval doesn't scale.

## Where judges fail

- Tasks requiring domain expertise the judge lacks.
- Tasks where the judge shares the agent's training biases.
- Tasks where the rubric is genuinely ambiguous.

## A real implementation

A team's voice-rubric judge:

- Calibrated against 200 human-scored examples.
- Agreement rate: 84%.
- Used for eval scoring on 500+ cases per run.
- Re-calibrated quarterly.

The judge handles volume the team couldn't manage with humans alone. The calibration discipline keeps it trustworthy.

## What we won't ship

**Judges** without calibration.

**Judges** with vague rubrics.

**Skipping the periodic audit.** Judges drift.

**Trusting judges in domains** where they share the agent's biases.

## Close

LLM-as-judge is a powerful eval pattern when calibrated. The rubric is specific. The agreement rate is measured. The audit cadence holds. Skip the calibration and the judge produces noise dressed as signal.
